Three approaches to engagement

Building off the three engagement factors: goals, spaces, and tools (Three engagement factors). In my personal model for engagement there are three approaches to engagement: Puzzle, exploration, and curiosity. Each approach combines two engagement factors.

Note: Two engagement factors alone might not be sufficient, there are many more factors at play such as the depth and complexity of those factors, how well they work together in tandem, and how "fun" the overall system is.

Playgrounds for the mind

Places of play, invention, and joy—the playground is not designed for an anti-purpose, instead of being designed with a specific goal in mind it is a place where people can come up with their own goals, create their own games, and have fun. Thinking back to my Three approaches to engagement, the playground takes the space of curiosity where you are given a space and a set of actions to explore and interact with this space. I am curious, what would it look like to design a playground for the mind? a place to explore and come up with ideas, create your own games, and explore conceptual space in a similar way that playgrounds make it fun to move about physical space.

Deliberate Practice for Knowledge Work is a Cycle of Engagement

Andy's focus is on exploration: writing, memorising, researching; and Simon's focus is on play: anti-repetition, and making. These two approaches do not contradict each other, they are but two sides of a triangle strengthening each other as part of a whole. From this I propose the third side of the triangle according to my Three approaches to engagement would be puzzle.

Cycle of Engagement

In circling around the question "what is engagement?" I have come up with Three approaches to engagement which are each formed of two out of Three engagement factors: goal, space, or verb. I further hypothesise that these three approaches to engagement come together to form a cycle of engagement which underlies not only why Games are inherently engaging mediums but also any creative work. Further, I believe that Deliberate Practice for Knowledge Work is a [[Cycle of Engagement]]

Games of strategy give the players a lot of agency in deciding how to organise and structure their environment. They take a predominantly puzzle approach with regards to my Three approaches to engagement

Hackathons are powerful learning experiences

Hackathons are more about the experience and the project than learning. The fact that you're also learning, practicing designing, art, sound effects, or even trying out an entirely new programming language and delving into documentation, all of this is subservient to the project in question. When I think back to my Three approaches to engagement I would say that hackathons take a sort of "competetive open-ended puzzle" approach to engagement. You have a goal, and a space (constrained time limit, some kind of interaction with other participants, theme), and you need to figure out the steps to achieve that goal.
